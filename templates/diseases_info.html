Understanding Common Eye Diseases

Detailed information on conditions detected by our AI model. Use the tabs below to explore each disease.

Glaucoma (‡§ï‡§æ‡§≤‡§æ ‡§Æ‡•ã‡§§‡§ø‡§Ø‡§æ)

Glaucoma is a group of eye conditions that damage the **optic nerve**, a vital bundle of nerves connecting the eye to the brain. This damage is often caused by abnormally high pressure in your eye, known as intraocular pressure (IOP).

It is often called the **"silent thief of sight"** because peripheral vision loss occurs so gradually that it often goes unnoticed until the disease is in an advanced stage. Early detection through regular comprehensive eye exams is crucial.

üî¨ Key Facts:

  • Glaucoma is the second leading cause of blindness worldwide.
  • The most common type, open-angle glaucoma, develops slowly over time.
  • Damage caused by glaucoma is permanent and cannot be reversed.

Symptoms:

  • Gradual loss of peripheral vision (often unnoticed initially).
  • Tunnel vision in advanced stages.
  • Severe eye pain, nausea, and sudden blurred vision (in acute angle-closure glaucoma).

Treatment & Management:

Treatment focuses on lowering eye pressure to prevent further damage. This is usually achieved with prescription **eye drops**, oral medications, or surgical procedures like laser treatment or traditional filtering surgery.

Cataract (‡§Æ‡•ã‡§§‡§ø‡§Ø‡§æ‡§¨‡§ø‡§Ç‡§¶)

A **cataract** is a clouding of the clear lens of the eye, which works like a camera lens to focus light onto the retina. When the lens becomes cloudy, it prevents light rays from passing through easily, resulting in blurry or dim vision. [Image of a human eye showing a cloudy lens (cataract)]

Most cataracts develop when aging or injury changes the tissue that makes up the eye's lens. Over time, proteins in the lens clump together, causing the lens to become opaque. While cataracts are common in older adults, they can also occur in younger people.

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Factors:

  • Age (the biggest risk factor).
  • Diabetes and obesity.
  • Excessive exposure to sunlight (UV radiation).
  • Smoking and previous eye injury or inflammation.

Symptoms:

  • Cloudy, blurry, or dim vision.
  • Increased difficulty with night vision.
  • Seeing "halos" around lights.
  • Fading or yellowing of colors.

Treatment:

Initially, vision change can be managed with stronger eyeglasses. However, the only effective and permanent treatment is **surgery** to remove the cloudy natural lens and replace it with an artificial intraocular lens (IOL).

Diabetic Retinopathy (‡§Æ‡§ß‡•Å‡§Æ‡•á‡§π ‡§∏‡§Ç‡§¨‡§Ç‡§ß‡•Ä ‡§∞‡•á‡§ü‡§ø‡§®‡•ã‡§™‡•à‡§•‡•Ä)

Diabetic Retinopathy is a severe complication of diabetes caused by damage to the tiny **blood vessels of the retina** (the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye). Uncontrolled high blood sugar damages these vessels, causing them to leak fluid or bleed, and in advanced stages, prompting the growth of new, abnormal vessels.

It is one of the leading causes of preventable blindness worldwide. Early detection and strict blood sugar control are the most critical steps to manage and prevent progression. [Image of a diabetic retina showing blood vessel damage and hemorrhages]

üî¨ Key Stages:

  • **Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR):** Early stage where microaneurysms (small bulges in vessels) and minor leaks occur.
  • **Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR):** Advanced stage where the retina attempts to compensate by growing new, fragile, abnormal blood vessels (neovascularization), which often bleed and cause scar tissue.

Symptoms:

  • Floaters (spots or strings drifting in your vision).
  • Blurred or distorted vision.
  • Fluctuating vision (vision changes from day to day).
  • Impaired color vision.
  • Dark or empty areas in your overall vision.

Treatment & Prevention:

The best prevention is excellent management of **blood glucose** and **blood pressure**. Treatment includes **anti-VEGF injections** (to stop vessel growth), **laser surgery** (photocoagulation to seal leaking vessels), or **vitrectomy** (to remove blood and scar tissue in advanced PDR).

Myopia (‡§®‡§ø‡§ï‡§ü ‡§¶‡•É‡§∑‡•ç‡§ü‡§ø ‡§¶‡•ã‡§∑ / Nearsightedness)

Myopia, commonly known as **nearsightedness**, is a refractive error where you can see objects close to you clearly, but distant objects appear blurry. It occurs when the **eyeball is too long** or the cornea is too curved, causing light to focus *in front* of the retina instead of directly *on* it.

Myopia is the most common vision problem globally, and its prevalence is increasing rapidly, particularly among children and young adults due to prolonged near-work activities like using screens.
